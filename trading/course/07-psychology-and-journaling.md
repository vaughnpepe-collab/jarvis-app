# 07 — Psychology & Journaling

The strategy is simple. *Executing it while real money swings around* is the hard
part. Every trader in this course was, above all, **emotionally disciplined**.
Druckenmiller could sit in cash for months. Soros cut losers without ego.
Lipschutz held through brutal volatility because his sizing let him. The edge is
useless if you can't run it calmly.

## The four account-killers

1. **Revenge trading.** Taking a trade *because you just lost*, to "win it back".
   This is how a 0.5%-risk method turns into a 5% gamble. Antidote: a **daily
   loss limit** (module 05) that is non-negotiable and automated where possible.
2. **Overtrading / boredom trades.** Forcing setups that miss a checklist line
   (module 04) because nothing has triggered and you're impatient. Antidote:
   redefine your job as *waiting for your pitch*, not *being in a trade*. No
   setup = winning.
3. **Moving stops / removing them.** Widening a stop because price is approaching
   it is choosing a bigger loss to avoid admitting a small one. Antidote: set it
   on entry and let the machine honour it. The stop is the thesis's invalidation,
   not a suggestion.
4. **Cutting winners early / fear of giving back.** Closing at +0.5R because
   you're scared, sabotaging the asymmetry the whole method depends on. Antidote:
   break-even-at-1R (module 06) makes most trades risk-free, which removes the
   fear that causes this.

Notice the pattern: **every psychological problem has a mechanical antidote.**
That is *why* we automate. The EA isn't there to predict the market — it's there
to stop *you* from sabotaging a perfectly good method.

## Process over outcome

You will have **losing trades that were perfectly executed** and **winning trades
that were reckless**. Judge yourself on the *first* axis only:

|  | Good process | Bad process |
|---|---|---|
| **Win** | Reinforce. This is the goal. | Got lucky — *do not* repeat. |
| **Loss** | A cost of doing business. Fine. | The real problem. Fix it. |

Any single trade is a coin flip with a weighted edge. Over hundreds of trades the
edge expresses itself — *if* you keep risk constant and the process clean. Your
job is to make the next 200 trades look like the same trade, taken the same way.

## The trading journal

If it isn't written down, it didn't happen and you won't learn from it. For every
trade, log:

- **Setup:** symbol, date/session, screenshot before & after.
- **Checklist:** which of the module-04 conditions were present? Did you skip any?
- **Risk:** % risked, stop distance, intended R:R.
- **Management:** did you follow the plan, or override? Why?
- **Result:** R-multiple (e.g. +3R, −1R), *not* just dollars.
- **One-line lesson.**

### Review weekly, not trade-by-trade

Each week, sort by **process score**, not P&L. Look for patterns:

- Are your *rule-following* trades net positive over time? If yes, the edge is
  real — your job is just compliance.
- Which **session / symbol / bias-strength** produces your best expectancy? Do
  more of that, cut the rest.
- What's your most common rule-break? **That** is your highest-value fix — worth
  more than any new indicator.

## Metrics that actually matter

Track these monthly:

- **Expectancy (average R per trade).** Positive and stable beats high and erratic.
- **Win rate & average win/loss in R.** Sanity-check against module 04's table.
- **Max drawdown (in R and %).** Can you stomach it? Size down if not.
- **Rule-adherence %.** The leading indicator of everything else.

A boring, consistent, positive-expectancy month with 100% rule-adherence is a
*better* result than a huge month full of overrides. The huge-override month is a
time bomb. Trade to still be here in five years.
